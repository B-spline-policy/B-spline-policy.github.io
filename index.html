TL;DR: Replace discrete action chunks with B-spline curves, for faster visuomotor policy.

Abstract: In this work, we present B-spline Policy (BSP), an action representation designed for accelerating robot manipulation policies. Rather than predicting discrete-time action chunks, BSP parameterizes actions as continuous B-spline curves defined by a set of knots and control points. This representation yields smooth, time-continuous trajectories that can be temporally scaled and executed by low-level controllers at higher frequencies and speeds. We show that B-spline–parameterized actions can be seamlessly integrated into standard policy learning pipelines by directly predicting B-spline parameters. Experiments on simulated and real-world tasks demonstrate that BSP significantly reduces task completion time, achieving substantial improvements over baseline methods while maintaining strong success rates.

Visuomotor policies were often slow

Speed remains a major bottleneck in visuomotor policies. Consider loading an object into a dishwasher: humans typically complete the task in a few seconds, whereas most of today's robotic systems require close to a minute.

One reason of this slow execution is action chunking: policies output fixed-rate waypoints, treating every moment the same. But manipulation is not uniform: robots should move fast in free space and slow down near contact making. Naively speeding up these waypoints often makes motion sparse, jerky, and hard to track.

To address these issues, we propose B-spline Policy, rather than predicting a chunk of fixed-rate waypoints, we parameterize action trajectories as continuous B-spline curves. This continuous representation enables temporal rescaling at execution time, allowing the policy to execute faster and more smoothly.

What are B-splines?

A B-spline defines a smooth curve (the red curve) by a set of knots (the grey diamonds) and control points (the blue dots). Instead of drawing straight lines between waypoints, a B-spline creates a smooth path that follows their overall trend. Think of it like bending a flexible ruler near waypoints instead of connecting them with sharp corners.

Fitting a B-spline

The diagram illustrates the iterative fitting of a B-spline to approximate a trajectory. Given a set of waypoints from a trajectory ( grey dots), it starts with only the two boundary knots (endpoints) and a coarse least-squares B-spline. It measures the reconstruction error against the target trajectory, drops a new knot where the error is worst, and refits. The loop repeats until the largest error falls at or below the selected threshold — then it starts over.

B-spline Policy

How do you learn a B-spline policy? Rather than predicting a fixed list of discrete-time waypoints, B-spline Policy parameterizes the action space as a continuous B-spline trajectory. Given a knot vector $U$ and a set of control points $C = \{c_0, \dots, c_N\}$, the continuous action at normalized time $u$ is

$$ \mathbf{a}(u) \;=\; \sum_{i=0}^{N} N_{i,p}(u)\, c_i, $$

where $N_{i,p}(u)$ is the $i$-th basis function of degree $p$ (we use cubic B-splines, $p=3$). This turns the policy output from a list of sampled poses into the parameters of a smooth curve that can be sampled at any control frequency.

Training. Each discrete demonstration is first converted into a continuous B-spline target. We fit a degree-$p$ curve within an error tolerance, inserting knots adaptively into the regions of largest reconstruction error (see Fitting a B-spline). Exploiting the local support of B-splines, the policy then predicts a near-future segment — both the control points and the knot values as one fixed-size vector $[U;\,C]$ — serving as a drop-in replacement for action-chunk prediction in backbones such as Diffusion Policy and ACT.

Inference and Execution. Because the output is a continuous curve, we decouple the policy rate from the control rate: the policy runs slowly while the controller samples the segment densely. Speed is set by rescaling time with a factor $n$, $\mathbf{a}_{\text{exec}}(t) = \mathbf{a}(n t)$, which preserves the trajectory's shape while traversing it faster — no retraining and none of the jerky motion of naively sped-up waypoints. For long-horizon tasks, a lightweight segment alignment step matches each new segment to the last executed action, keeping consecutive segments continuous at high speed.

Experiments

We integrate B-spline Policy into two imitation-learning backbones — Diffusion Policy (Diff. + BSP) and ACT (Reg. + BSP) — and evaluate it on three real-world tasks that span distinct challenges: Cube Picking (precise object manipulation), Table Cleaning (long-horizon rearrangement), and Speed Stacking (bimanual, contact-rich), along with the Push-T, RoboMimic, and RoboCasa simulation benchmarks. Across configurations BSP consistently reduces task completion time while preserving or improving success rate — for example, cutting Table Cleaning time roughly in half at a 4× speedup — and produces visibly smoother motion.
